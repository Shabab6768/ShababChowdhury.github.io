Evaluating Ocean Color in the NASA-GISS Climate Model

Conventional wisdom thinks the color of the ocean is blue. However, that is only half the truth. The color humans see is the color of the sunlight that is reflected by the ocean surface after some of the wavelengths of the incident light have been absorbed and scattered by phytoplankton and other particles in the ocean. NASA remote sensing measures ocean color using satellites and aircrafts. Argo floats measure ocean color through a fleet of robotic instruments that move up and down between the ocean surface and a mid-water level. Knowing the ocean color reveals information about phytoplankton and how it changes during the seasons in different geographical regions. Phytoplankton plays a crucial role in the global carbon cycle as it removes carbon dioxide naturally from the atmosphere to use it for photosynthesis. This process provides half of the oxygen in the atmosphere that humans breathe. Some important measures of the light in the ocean which relate to phytoplankton are the photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) and the inherent optical properties (IOP) such as light absorption and backscatter light. In this project different regions of the ocean are observed to see how these properties behave.
